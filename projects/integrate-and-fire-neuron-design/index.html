Integrate-and-Fire Neuron VLSI Design

A full-custom digital integrate-and-fire neuron designed from gate-level schematic through DRC/LVS-clean physical layout in 32 nm CMOS, achieving 391 µm² area, 5.79 ps worst-case propagation delay, and 0.5 ns minimum clock period — verified by HSPICE transient simulation and StarRC parasitic extraction across both pre- and post-layout netlists.

Overview

This project implemented a digital integrate-and-fire (IF) neuron — a fundamental building block in neuromorphic computing — through the complete VLSI design flow from gate-level schematic to fabrication-ready physical layout. Designed in 32 nm CMOS using Cadence Virtuoso, the project followed a rigorous tape-out discipline: transistor-level schematic capture, pre-layout HSPICE transient simulation to validate functional correctness, full-custom physical layout with manual transistor placement and routing, post-layout parasitic extraction via StarRC, and final sign-off with DRC and LVS verification. A supporting D flip-flop was also designed through the same full-custom flow to provide the digital storage element required by the neuron's firing logic.

The critical design challenges were achieving compact area without violating design-rule constraints, closing timing under worst-case post-layout parasitics, and eliminating all DRC violations while maintaining LVS-clean correspondence between schematic and layout — all within the strict 32 nm node geometry rules.

Design flow

The full design flow proceeded across four structured stages, each building on verified output from the previous stage.

  • Schematic capture and pre-layout simulation: The IF neuron and D flip-flop were designed at the transistor level in Cadence Virtuoso Schematic Editor. HSPICE transient simulations were run against the pre-layout netlist to verify functional correctness — accumulation behavior, threshold firing, and reset logic — before committing to layout.
  • Full-custom physical layout: Layouts were drawn manually in Cadence Virtuoso Layout Suite at the 32 nm node. Transistor-pair matching was applied across differential signal paths to minimize systematic mismatch. Signal shielding was introduced on critical nets to reduce substrate coupling. Routing was planned parasitic-aware from the start — minimizing wire length on timing-critical paths and using metal layer selection to bound capacitive loading.
  • Parasitic extraction and post-layout simulation: StarRC extracted the complete RC parasitic network from the finished layout. Post-layout netlists were re-simulated in HSPICE to quantify the timing degradation introduced by physical parasitics, establishing the worst-case propagation delay under realistic loading conditions.
  • DRC and LVS sign-off: Design Rule Check was run to verify all minimum spacing, width, enclosure, and density rules at 32 nm. Layout Versus Schematic was run to confirm one-to-one correspondence between the physical layout and the original transistor-level schematic — eliminating open nets, short circuits, and device parameter mismatches before sign-off.

Layout techniques applied

Several physical design techniques were applied to achieve timing closure and DRC/LVS clean-out under 32 nm constraints.

  • Transistor-pair matching: Interdigitated transistor placement used for symmetric differential paths to reduce systematic offset from process gradients.
  • Signal shielding: Critical signal wires routed with adjacent grounded shields to suppress capacitive coupling from aggressor nets.
  • Parasitic-aware routing: Metal layer selection and wire sizing driven by timing sensitivity — shorter, wider routes on critical paths; longer, thin routes permissible on non-critical nets.
  • Compact placement: Transistors placed with minimum legal spacing across all 32 nm rules, achieving 391 µm² total layout area for the IF neuron cell.

Measured outcomes

  • Achieved 391 µm² total layout area by applying compact transistor placement with minimum legal 32 nm spacing, transistor-pair interdigitation, and manual routing optimized for area within design-rule constraints — verified by DRC sign-off in Cadence Virtuoso.
  • Achieved 5.79 ps worst-case propagation delay and 0.5 ns minimum clock period by closing timing under post-layout parasitic loading — quantified by HSPICE transient re-simulation of the StarRC-extracted netlist and compared against pre-layout simulation to measure parasitic impact.
  • Delivered DRC-clean, LVS-passing physical sign-off for both the integrate-and-fire neuron and the D flip-flop by eliminating all design-rule violations through transistor-pair matching, signal shielding, and parasitic-aware routing, and by confirming zero netlist discrepancies between layout and schematic in Cadence Virtuoso LVS.
  • Validated timing closure under worst-case parasitic conditions by running pre-layout and post-layout HSPICE simulations against the same testbench, with StarRC-extracted parasitics quantifying the increase in propagation delay from ideal to physical routing — confirming the 0.5 ns clock period constraint was met post-extraction.

Why integrate-and-fire?

The integrate-and-fire neuron model is the foundational digital primitive in neuromorphic circuit design. It mimics biological neuron behavior — accumulating weighted input signals, firing an output spike when a threshold is crossed, and resetting — using minimal digital logic, making it a key target for area- and power-constrained VLSI implementation. Implementing it through a complete full-custom flow, rather than synthesizing from RTL, develops the layout discipline, parasitic reasoning, and DRC/LVS fluency required for custom analog-mixed-signal and neuromorphic chip design.
