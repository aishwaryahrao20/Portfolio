EV Power Module Packaging: From Conventional to Next-Gen Integration

A research project analyzing next-generation packaging architectures, materials, and thermal topologies for wide-bandgap semiconductors in electric vehicle applications.

Overview

This research project examined thermal, mechanical, and electrical constraints that limit current power module packaging, then evaluated next-generation architectures for SiC and GaN devices in EV applications.

What we covered

  • Analyzed thermo-mechanical failure modes in conventional wire-bonded, single-sided-cooled power modules, including solder fatigue, CTE mismatch, and wire-bond lift-off.
  • Evaluated advanced ceramic substrates such as AlN and Si3N4 AMB, plus emerging organic dielectrics for thermal performance, reliability, and cost tradeoffs.
  • Assessed double-sided cooling and baseplate-free architectures against thermal resistance and power-cycling lifetime metrics.
  • Reviewed wirebondless interconnect innovations such as copper clips, direct lead bonding, and PCB-embedded die.

Engineering focus

  • Mapped the failure chain from fast SiC switching to parasitic stray inductance, voltage overshoot, and device stress.
  • Compared thermal-resistance and power-cycling trade-offs across packaging and cooling strategies.
  • Connected substrate material choices to EV traction-inverter reliability and cost.
Project details
  • Duration: Jan - May 2025
  • Associated with: Arizona State University
  • Project type: Team Research / Power Electronics
  • Focus: SiC/GaN packaging and thermal management
